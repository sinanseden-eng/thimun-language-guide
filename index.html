Master the Art of Parliamentary Discourse

üéØ Golden Rule

Always speak in third person!

Never say "I think..." ‚Üí Say "The delegate of [Country] believes..."

‚≠ê Mastering the Third Person: The Rule in Action ‚ñº

This is the most important rule of formal debate. You are not representing yourself; you are representing an entire country. Using "I" or "my" breaks this formality. Here are common mistakes and how to fix them.

Common Situation ‚ùå Don't Say This (First Person) ‚úÖ Say This (Third Person)
Stating an Opinion "I think we should pass this resolution." "The delegate of Japan believes the committee should pass this resolution."
Asking a Question "My question for you is: where will you get the money?" "The delegate of Kenya asks the delegate of France: how will this fund be financed?"
Making a Motion "I want to motion to close debate." "The delegate of Germany motions to close debate."
Referring to another Delegate "I agree with what he just said." "The delegate of South Africa concurs with the previous speaker."
Referring to a group "All of us here want peace." "All delegates present wish to see a peaceful resolution."
Stating National Policy "My country has always fought for human rights." "The nation of Canada has a long-standing policy of supporting human rights."
Proposing an Amendment "I wrote an amendment to add a new clause." "The delegate of Brazil has submitted an amendment to add a new operative clause."
Responding to a Question "To answer your question, my plan will work because..." "In response to the delegate's query, this plan will succeed because..."
Expressing Emotion "I am shocked by this proposal." "The delegation of France is shocked by this proposal."
Correcting a Fact "You are wrong. The statistic I found says..." "The delegate of Russia would like to correct a factual inaccuracy. The correct statistic is..."
Yielding Time "I'm done. I yield my time to the chair." "The delegate of Egypt yields the floor back to the Chair."
Referring to Personal Knowledge "In a book I read, it said..." "The delegate of China would like to bring to the committee's attention that..."
Asking for a Point "I have a Point of Information." "Point of Information to the speaker." (Or just raise placard)
Disagreeing Strongly "I completely disagree with your entire speech." "The delegate of the United States respectfully but firmly disagrees with the position of the previous speaker."
Addressing the Chair "Can you tell me if this is allowed?" "Point of Parliamentary Enquiry: The delegate wishes to know if this motion is in order."
Referring to an Ally "My allies and I all agree that..." "The delegate of Germany, along with its fellow EU member states, agrees that..."
Refuting an Argument "That's a bad idea because it won't work." "The delegation of India finds this proposal to be unfeasible because..."
Making a Suggestion "Maybe we should try to create a new fund." "The delegate of Nigeria suggests the creation of a new fund."
Asking for Clarification "I don't understand what you mean." "Could the delegate of Australia please clarify what is meant by 'regional mechanisms'?"
Referring to the Resolution "I like the first part of my resolution, but not the second." "The delegate of Brazil supports the preambulatory clauses but has concerns about the operative clauses."

üôã‚Äç‚ôÄÔ∏è Points in THIMUN

1Ô∏è‚É£ Point of Information (to the Speaker) ‚ñº

Purpose: Ask questions to the speaker who currently has the floor, but ONLY if they have indicated willingness to yield to points of information.

‚ö†Ô∏è THIMUN Rule: Cannot interrupt a speaker. Follow-up questions from the same questioner are usually not in order.
üìç Example: Asking for Elaboration
Chair: "Thank you, delegate of India. To what point do you yield your time?"
Delegate of India: "The delegate of India yields to Points of Information."
Chair: "Are there any Points of Information on the floor?"
(You raise your placard and are recognized)
You (Kenya): "Point of Information to the speaker. The delegate of India mentioned a new fund. Could the delegate please elaborate on how this fund would be financed?"
Delegate of India: "The delegate thanks the delegate of Kenya for the question. The fund would be financed through..."
2Ô∏è‚É£ Point of Information to the Chair ‚ñº

Purpose: Ask the Chair questions about any matter related to the committee's business or for clarification on non-procedural issues (e.g., debate time, lunch schedule).

üìç Example: Asking About Time
Chair: "We will now move to debate on the draft resolution. Are there any points or motions?"
(You raise your placard)
You (Egypt): "Point of Information to the Chair."
Chair: "The delegate of Egypt."
You: "Could the Chair inform the committee of the debate time allocated for this resolution?"
Chair: "The Chair proposes 30 minutes of debate time. Seeing no objections, it is set."
3Ô∏è‚É£ Point of Parliamentary Enquiry ‚ñº

Purpose: Ask the Chair questions *specifically* concerning the rules of procedure.

üìç Example: Motion Clarification
Chair: "Thank you, delegate. Are there any points or motions?"
(You raise your placard)
You (Nigeria): "Point of Parliamentary Enquiry."
Chair: "Yes, delegate of Nigeria?"
You: "Is it in order to move the Previous Question at this time?"
Chair: "That is correct, delegate. A motion to close debate would be in order."
4Ô∏è‚É£ Point of Order ‚ñº

Purpose: Call attention to a procedural error (when the rules of procedure are being violated by the Chair or a delegate).

‚ö†Ô∏è THIMUN Rule: Points of Order may ONLY relate to procedural matters, not speech content. Use this sparingly.
üìç Example: Incorrect Debate Time
Chair: "The Chair proposes 20 minutes of debate time for this amendment."
(You raise your placard)
You (Japan): "Point of Order."
Chair: "State your point, delegate of Japan."
You: "According to the conference rules, debate on amendments is limited to a maximum of 10 minutes."
Chair: "The point is well taken. The Chair stands corrected. Debate time is set at 10 minutes."
5Ô∏è‚É£ Point of Personal Privilege ‚ñº

Purpose: Address issues of personal comfort (e.g., room temperature, noise) that prevent you from participating.

‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL THIMUN Rule: This is the ONLY point that can interrupt a speaker, but *only* if the speech is inaudible.
üìç Example: Inaudible Speaker (CAN INTERRUPT)
Delegate of Italy: (speaking very quietly) "...and the delegate believes..."
You (Argentina): "Point of Personal Privilege!"
Chair: "The speaker will pause. State your point, delegate of Argentina."
You: "The delegate cannot hear the speaker. The delegate kindly requests that the speaker speak more loudly."
Chair: "Delegate of Italy, please speak into the microphone. You may continue."

‚öñÔ∏è Motions & Yields

ü§ù Yielding (Giving Up Your Time) ‚ñº

After your speech, the Chair will ask how you yieldTo give up your speaking time. your time. You have three main options.

Option 1: To the Chair (Most Common)
You: "The delegate yields the floor back to the Chair."
What it means: You are done and do not want to answer questions.
Option 2: To Questions
You: "The delegate yields to Points of Information."
What it means: You are opening the floor for other delegates to ask you questions.
Option 3: To Another Delegate
You: "The delegate yields the floor to the delegate of Argentina."
What it means: You give the rest of your time to another delegate (must be pre-arranged). THIMUN Rule: This can only be done once consecutively. The next delegate must yield back to the Chair.
üó≥Ô∏è Motion to Close Debate (Previous QuestionThe formal name for the motion to end debate and vote.) ‚ñº

Purpose: To end the debate on the current topic (resolution or amendment) and move immediately to a vote.

üìç Example: Closing Debate
Chair: "Are there any points or motions on the floor?"
You (Germany): "Honourable Chair, the delegate of Germany moves the Previous Question."
Chair: "That motion is in order. Are there any seconds?"
(Other Delegates): "Second!"
Chair: "Are there any objections?"
(Another Delegate): "Objection!"
Chair: "We will have two speakers for and two speakers against this motion..."
üóìÔ∏è Motion to Adjourn the DebateTo "table" or pause debate on the current topic, moving to the next item. ‚ñº

Purpose: To stop debate on the current topic (resolution) and "table" it, meaning it is set aside to be potentially debated later, after all other resolutions.

Rules: This motion is debatable (usually one speaker for, one against) and requires a simple majority to pass.

üìç Example: Tabling a Resolution
You (Argentina): "Honourable Chair, the delegate of Argentina motions to adjourn the debate on Resolution 1.1."
Chair: "That motion is in order. Are there any seconds? ... Objections? ... We will have one speaker for and one against this motion."
‚è±Ô∏è Motion to Extend Debate Time ‚ñº

Purpose: To add more time for debate on the current topic (resolution or amendment).

Rules: This motion is not debatable and is decided at the Chair's discretion. It requires a "second."

üìç Example: Extending Time
Chair: "Debate time has elapsed. Are there any points or motions?"
You (Canada): "Honourable Chair, the delegate motions to extend debate time by 10 minutes."
Chair: "Are there any seconds? ... Seeing seconds, the Chair will entertain this. We will move to a vote..."
üîÑ Motion to Reconsider a Resolution ‚ñº

Purpose: To bring a resolution that has already been passed or failed back to the floor for a new debate and vote.

Rules: This can only be done after all other resolutions on the agenda have been debated. It is not debatable and requires a two-thirds (2/3) majority to pass.

üìã Other Motions & Objections ‚ñº
Motion to Refer to Another Forum
Purpose: Used if a clause is outside the committee's power (e.g., uses "Demands" in the General Assembly). It's discouraged; chairs prefer amending the clause (e.g., to "Urges the Security Council to...").
You (Peru): "Honourable Chair, the delegate of Peru motions to refer this resolution to the Security Council, as operative clause 2 uses language such as 'Demands' which is outside the purview of the General Assembly."
Withdrawing a Resolution
Rule: The main submitter can motion to withdraw their resolution, but it requires *all* co-submitters to agree to it.
Delegate of Japan (Main Submitter): "Honourable Chair, the delegate of Japan motions to withdraw Resolution 1.1 from the floor."
Objection to the Main Motion
Purpose: To object to the *entire resolution* being debated, often on grounds that it violates national sovereignty. It is strongly discouraged as it's destructive to debate. Requires a 2/3 majority.
You (China): "Honourable Chair, the delegate of China objects to the main motion."

üîë Key Rules & Terms

üó≥Ô∏è Voting Procedure: Who Can Vote? ‚ñº

A critical distinction exists between "procedural" and "substantive" votes.

Substantive Votes (Resolutions & Amendments)
Only Member States (countries) can vote on resolutions and amendments.
Observers (e.g., NGOs like the Red Cross, or non-member states) CANNOT vote on substantive matters.
Procedural Votes (Motions)
ALL delegates (Member States *and* Observers) can vote on procedural motions.
Example: An NGO (Observer) cannot vote on an amendment, but *can* vote on a "Motion to Extend Debate Time" for that amendment.
üì£ Right of ReplyA right to respond if your country has been insulted. Granted by the Chair. ‚ñº

Purpose: This is not a "Point" but a formal right. A delegate can request a Right of Reply if their country or delegation has been insulted by a speaker.

üìç Example: Responding to an Insult
Delegate of X: "...which is why the warmongering policy of Country Y is destabilizing the region."
(You, Delegate of Y, send a note to the Chair)
Chair: "Thank you, delegate of X. The Chair has received a request for a Right of Reply from the delegate of Y. You have the floor for 30 seconds."
You (Y): "The delegate of Y wishes to state that our policies are for self-defense, not "warmongering," and we find such language unhelpful to diplomatic debate."
üìã Roll Call & QuorumThe minimum number of delegates (usually 1/3) needed to start debate or vote. ‚ñº
Roll Call
What it is: The attendance check at the beginning of every session.
Chair: "The committee will come to order. We will now begin Roll Call. Delegates, please respond 'Present' or 'Present and Voting'..."
Quorum
What it is: The minimum number of delegates (usually one-third) that must be present for formal debate to begin and for votes to be valid. The Chair establishes quorum during the first roll call.

üí° THIMUN Pro Tips

  • Respect the Chair: You must respect the decisions of the Chair at all times. Their rulings are final.
  • Stand Up: You must always stand when speaking, whether giving a speech or raising a point.
  • Jacket On: Whenever a delegate has the floor (i.e., is speaking), they must have their suit jacket on and buttoned.
  • Be Diplomatic: Always be courteous. You may attack a country's *policy*, but never the *delegate*.
  • No Interruptions: A speaker may *never* be interrupted, except for an audibility-related Point of Personal Privilege.
  • Voting is Sacred: Once the Chair has announced the start of voting, no interruptions are allowed except for a Point of Order related to the vote itself.

A Simple Guide to Writing a Resolution

(THIMUN Style)

What is a Resolution?

A resolution is a single, long sentence that proposes a solution to a problem. It's like a formal recommendation from the committee.

It is split into two main parts:

  • Preambulatory Clauses (The "WHY"): Explains the background of the problem and why you're concerned.
  • Operative Clauses (The "HOW"): The action plan. This is what you want the UN and other countries to *do*.

Part 1: The Header

This is the "address" of your resolution. It must be 100% correct.

FORUM: General Assembly First Committee (Your committee)
QUESTION OF: The role of diamonds in fueling conflict... (The topic, exactly as written on the agenda)
SUBMITTED BY: Saint Vincent and the Grenadines (The main author)
CO-SUBMITTERS: Argentina, Australia, Canada, France... (Other delegations that want to see this debated. List them in alphabetical order.)

Part 2: The Preamble (The "WHY")

This is the introduction. You are explaining the problem and setting the context. Remember these key rules:

  • Starts with the full committee name in all caps (e.g., THE DISARMAMENT COMMISSION,)
  • Clause starters are italicized.
  • Every clause ends with a comma (,).

Here are 12 common starters to choose from:

Aware of
Bearing in mind
Deeply concerned
Declaring
Emphasizing
Expressing its satisfaction
Guided by
Having examined
Keeping in mind
Noting with regret
Reaffirming
Recalling
...
Aware of that 4% of diamonds produced are classified as "conflict or blood diamonds",

Recognizing the Kimberley Process Certification Scheme (KPCS) as an internationally recognized system,

Further recognizing that the KPCS is lacking a comprehensive system for monitoring,

Part 3: The Operative (The "HOW")

This is your action plan. You are explaining the specific solutions. Remember these key rules:

  • Clauses are numbered (1, 2, 3...).
  • Clause starters are underlined.
  • Every clause ends with a semicolon (;).
  • The VERY LAST clause ends with a period (.). This is the only period in the entire resolution.

Here are 12 common starters (for non-Security Council committees):

Approves
Asks
Authorizes
Calls for
Calls upon
Encourages
Expresses its hope
Further invites
Recommends
Reminds
Requests
Supports
...
1. Calls for all relevant Member States to sign and ratify the KPCS to promote cooperation;

2. Further calls for all relevant nations which are withholding signature... to request funds or low interest loans from intergovernmental organizations (IGOs) such as the International Monetary Fund (IMF) and the World Bank;

3. Asks for the creation of the Collation for Stopping Conflict Diamonds (CSCD). <-- This is the last clause!

Part 4: The Tricky Rules (Made Easy)

This is what most people get wrong. Here's how to get it right!

üí° Acronyms (e.g., KPCS)

You must write out the full name the first time you use it, followed by the acronym in parentheses. After that, you can just use the acronym.

...the Kimberley Process Certification Scheme (KPCS)...

(Later in the resolution...)
...to sign and ratify the KPCS...

üö´ No Repeating Starters

You cannot use the same clause starter twice (e.g., you can't have two clauses starting with Recognizing).

The Fix: Just add "Further" to the beginning!

Noting that smuggling is bad,
Further noting that illicit diamonds are a problem,

Sub-Clauses (a, b, c)

If an operative clause has multiple parts, you use sub-clauses. Introduce them with a colon (:).

Punctuation Rule: The sub-clauses themselves have no punctuation at the end. Only the *last* one gets the semicolon for the whole clause.

3. Asks for the creation of the CSCD... in order to stop illicit diamond transportation... by:
a. monitoring who is buying and selling
b. ensuring the methods are legal
c. sending submitted proof of diamond proceeds; <-- Semicolon goes here!

Sub-Sub-Clauses (i, ii, iii)

You can even go one level deeper! The same punctuation rule applies.

List Rule: You can't have a list of one. If you have an `a.`, you MUST have a `b.`. If you have a `i.`, you MUST have a `ii.`.

4. Urges a customs checking system including:
a. a monitoring system... in order to:
i. check the amount of "clean" diamonds
ii. make sure certificates are genuine
b. cooperation between customs and INTERPOL;

Here is a full resolution with remarks explaining each part, based on the PDF you provided.

FORUM: Disarmament Sub-Commission 1
QUESTION OF: The role of diamonds in fueling conflict: breaking the link between the illicit transaction of rough diamonds and armed conflict as a contribution to prevention and settlement of conflicts
SUBMITTED BY: Saint Vincent and the Grenadines
CO-SUBMITTERS: Argentina, Australia, Canada, France, Germany, India, Indonesia, Japan, Mexico, Saudi Arabia, South Africa, T√ºrkiye, United Kingdom, United States of America
The Header must be perfect. The `FORUM` and `QUESTION OF` must match the official agenda exactly. `CO-SUBMITTERS` must be listed in alphabetical order, and country names must be the official ones recognized by the UN.
THE DISARMAMENT COMMISSION,

Noting that the smuggling of illicit rough diamonds accounts for over 50 percent of the diamond trade and it is estimated by the Government Gold and Diamond Office that the total production of rough illicit diamonds amounts to 100 million USD per year,

Further noting illicit rough diamonds as diamonds that are uncut and unpolished but are used as "conflict or blood diamonds" to finance conflicts to overthrow governments,

Aware that 4% of diamonds produced are classified as "conflict or blood diamonds," which is enough to purchase weapons to destabilize the entire African continent,

Defining "conflict or blood diamonds" as gems which are found in regions that are not under the control of the democratically elected ruler or government, or diamonds used by business or non-state forces to fund movements of human rights abuses against civilians,
Preambulatory Clauses (The "WHY")
Notice how this section sets the stage. It defines the problem ("conflict or blood diamonds"), states the scale (100 million USD), and explains why it's a problem (used to finance conflicts).
  • Clause starters are italicized.
  • Every clause ends with a comma (,).
  • The committee name ("THE DISARMAMENT COMMISSION,") is in all caps.
  • No repeated starters: `Noting` is followed by `Further noting`.
Recognizing the Kimberley Process Certification Scheme (KPCS) as an internationally recognized certification system, established in 2003 by the United Nations (UN) General Assembly to establish national import and export standards of rough diamonds,

Further recognizing that the KPCS is lacking a comprehensive system for monitoring where diamonds are mined, how they are mined, what the proceeds are funding and how diamonds are domestically transported, which influences a government's control over conflicts and can possibly promote illicit diamond trading,
Acronyms (KPCS, UN)
This is the first time "Kimberley Process Certification Scheme" and "United Nations" are used, so they are written out in full, followed by their acronyms in parentheses. Now, the resolution can just use "KPCS" and "UN".
1. Calls for all relevant Member States to sign and ratify the KPCS to promote cooperation and awareness of trade;

2. Further calls for all relevant nations which are withholding signature and ratification of the KPCS due to financial reasons, to request funds or low interest loans from intergovernmental organizations (IGOs) such as the International Monetary Fund (IMF) and the World Bank;
Operative Clauses (The "HOW")
Now the action begins. These are the solutions.
  • Clauses are numbered (1, 2, 3...).
  • Clause starters are underlined.
  • Every clause ends with a semicolon (;).
  • Notice the use of `Further calls for` to avoid repeating `Calls for`.
  • Acronyms like "IGO" and "IMF" are written out in full the first time.
3. Asks for the creation of the Collation for Stopping Conflict Diamonds (CSCD), a United Nations Organization (UNO), which would work with the relevant Member States... in order to stop illicit diamond transportation both domestically and internationally level by:
a. monitoring in their respective countries who is buying and selling the diamonds
b. ensuring the methods by which the diamonds are bought and mined are legal according to the KPCS's definition of "clean" diamonds as well as ensuring compliance with the Geneva Convention
c. sending a submitted proof of each country's and mining companies' diamond proceeds to ensure that they are not funding armed conflicts
d. sending a member of the CSCD and of the UNMOVIC in the first year following a nation's ratification of the KPCS, to observe the changes, thus ensuring transparency;
Sub-Clauses (a, b, c)
When a clause has a list of actions, use sub-clauses.
  • Introduce the list with a colon (:).
  • Sub-clauses start with lowercase letters (a, b, c...).
  • Only the *last* sub-clause (d) gets the semicolon (;) that ends the *entire* clause 3.
4. Urges that the monitoring system of CSCD and Member States that have signed the KPCS allow and implement a customs checking system in each county including:
a. a monitoring system using modern measuring and weighing instruments, financed if needed by the IMF, in order to:
i. check that the amount and quality of "clean" diamonds stated on the certificate is received
ii. also make sure that the certificates received are genuine and include all necessary information
iii. confiscate any unregistered diamonds and the revenue of the illegal sales which will then be used for the CSCD
b. the cooperation between the custom checking system, Member States and International Criminal Police Organization (INTERPOL), in order to allocate a punishment for offenders who are illegally smuggling diamonds by:
i. withholding certificates for international and domestic transportation of the businesses and Member States
ii. investigating other past certificates issued by the business or persons that sent the certificate in order to prevent more illegal smuggling
iii. implementing other penalties suggested by the CSCD and any other relevant body;
Sub-Sub-Clauses (i, ii, iii)
You can go even deeper for more detail.
  • Sub-clause `a.` and `b.` introduce their lists with a colon (:).
  • Sub-sub-clauses use lowercase Roman numerals (i, ii, iii...).
  • The punctuation rule is the same: only the *very last* item (`b. iii.`) gets the final semicolon (;) for all of clause 4.
5. Suggests the creation of an online database...;

6. Further suggests the creation of an internationally accepted KPCS form...;

7. Promotes publicizing of verified investigations and sanctions run by the CSCD on multinational corporations engaged in the purchase of blood diamonds, to jeopardize their reputation.
The Final Clause
The very last operative clause (clause 7) is the only place in the *entire document* (after the header) that ends with a period (.). This officially ends the one-sentence resolution.

Pro-Tip: Avoid "wasting" your final clause on a phrase like "Remains seized on the matter." Make your last clause an action.

Common Chair Phrases & Dialogues

                
As a chair, you must control the flow of debate using precise parliamentary language. Here are some of the most essential phrases.


                
Starting Debate

                

                    ‚ÄúThe House will come to order.‚Äù
                    
Used to start the session or regain control if delegates are talking.

                

                

                    ‚ÄúThe chair calls upon the delegate of [...] (the submitter) to read the resolution to the House.‚Äù
                

                

                    ‚ÄúThe House has heard the resolution. Is there a second?‚Äù
                    
Delegate: "Second!"
Chair: "Are there any objections? ... Seeing none, the motion is in order."

                

                

                    ‚ÄúThe chair fixes a debate time of 10 minutes for and 10 minutes against the motion.‚Äù
                


                
Managing Speakers

                

                    ‚ÄúThe delegate of [...] has the floor.‚Äù
                    
This formally gives the delegate permission to speak.

                

                

                    ‚ÄúThe chair recognizes the delegate of‚Ä¶‚Äù.
                    
Used to select the next delegate to speak or raise a point.

                

                

                    ‚ÄúAre there any further points on the floor?‚Äù
                    
Used to ask for motions or points when no one is speaking.

                

                 

                    ‚ÄúAre there any further points of information to this speaker?‚Äù
                    
Used after a delegate has answered a Point of Information, to see if others have questions.

                

                

                    ‚ÄúWill the speaker please make his/her concluding remarks.‚Äù
                    
A polite way to tell a speaker their time is almost up and they need to finish.

                

                

                    ‚ÄúDebate time... has expired. Will the speaker please yield the floor.‚Äù
                    
The formal way to stop a speaker whose time has run out.

                


                
Managing Points

                

                    ‚ÄúTo what point does the delegate of [...] rise?‚Äù
                    
Delegate: "Point of Parliamentary Enquiry."
Chair: "Please rise and state your point."

                

                

                    ‚ÄúAll points are out of order until the speaker has concluded his/her speech.‚Äù
                    
Used if a delegate tries to raise a non-urgent point while someone is speaking.

                

                 

                    ‚ÄúWill you please state your point in a form of a question.‚Äù
                    
Used when a delegate is making a statement instead of asking a question during a Point of Information.

                

                

                    ‚ÄúWill you please repeat/rephrase your question.‚Äù
                    
Used if the speaker or Chair did not understand a delegate's point.

                

                

                    ‚ÄúYour point is (not) well taken.‚Äù
                    
The formal way to accept or reject a Point of Order.

                


                
Moving to Vote

                 

                    ‚ÄúThe chair proposes an extension of debate time by 5 minutes for and 5 minutes against the motion.‚Äù
                

                

                    ‚ÄúThe debate is now closed. We will move into voting procedures. All points are out of order.‚Äù
                    
This is a critical announcement. It makes all note-passing and discussion illegal until voting is over.

                

                

                    ‚ÄúThe motion will now be put to the vote.‚Äù
                

                

                    ‚ÄúWill all those in favour of the resolution/amendment, please raise their placards.‚Äù
                

                

                    ‚ÄúWill all those opposed/against... please raise their placards.‚Äù
                

                

                    ‚ÄúWill all those abstaining, please raise their placards.‚Äù
                

                

                    ‚ÄúWith X votes in favour, Y votes against and Z votes abstaining, the motion/resolution/amendment has passed/failed.‚Äù
                

                

                    ‚ÄúClapping is (not) in order.‚Äù
                    
Used to maintain decorum after a vote. (In THIMUN, clapping is usually only in order after the entire debate is adjourned.)

How to Structure a Persuasive Speech

            


            
            

                
How to Structure a Speech (60-90 Seconds)

                

                    
1. Opening (10 sec): State your position clearly.
"Thank you, Chair. The delegation of [Country] rises in strong support of this resolution."

                    

                    
2. Key Points (40-60 sec): Give 2-3 strong arguments.
"First, this resolution addresses... Second, the funding mechanism in Clause 3 is..."

                    

                    
3. Closing (10 sec): Reinforce your position and call to action.
"This resolution is vital for climate justice. We urge all delegations to vote in favor."

Topic: Establishing a Global Climate Adaptation Fund

                    

                        
Key Operative Clauses:

                        
    
                            
  • Establishes a $100 billion annual fund for climate adaptation in developing nations.
    • 
                            
  • Requires developed nations to contribute based on historical emissions.
    • 
                            
  • Creates an independent committee to allocate funds based on vulnerability.
    • 
                        

                    

                    

                        

                            
‚úÖ Speech FOR (e.g., Bangladesh)

                            
"Thank you, Chair. The delegation of Bangladesh rises in strong support of this resolution.

                            
(Moral/Rights-Based Argument) First, this fund addresses a fundamental injustice. Developing nations contribute least to climate change yet suffer most from its impacts. Bangladesh faces annual flooding that displaces millions, yet we produced less than 0.5% of global emissions. This resolution recognizes the principle of common but differentiated responsibility.

                            
(Economic/Data-Based Argument) Second, the $100 billion figure is not arbitrary‚Äîit reflects the actual adaptation costs identified by the UN Environment Programme. Current funding reaches only $20 billion annually, leaving a critical gap that costs lives and livelihoods.

                            
This resolution is not charity‚Äîit is climate justice. We urge all delegations to vote in favor. Thank you."

                        

                        

                            
‚ùå Speech AGAINST (e.g., Singapore)

                            
"Thank you, Chair. The delegation of Singapore must respectfully oppose this resolution, despite sharing concerns about climate adaptation.

                            
(Economic Argument) First, the $100 billion annual commitment is economically unrealistic. Many developed nations face their own fiscal constraints. The resolution provides no timeline for contribution increases, risking economic strain and broken promises.

                            
(Implementation/Vagueness Argument) Second, the contribution formula based solely on historical emissions is flawed. It ignores current emission levels and economic capacity. Nations like China, now the world's largest emitter, would contribute less than smaller developed nations. This formula is neither fair nor effective.

                            
We need climate finance reform, but this resolution's approach is financially unsustainable and diplomatically divisive. We urge delegates to vote against. Thank you."

                        

                    

                


                
                

                    
Topic: Protecting Citizens' Data Privacy in the Digital Age

                    

                        
Key Operative Clauses:

  • Mandates that tech companies obtain explicit consent before collecting personal data.
    •

    • 
                            
  • Establishes an international data protection enforcement agency.

‚úÖ Speech FOR (e.g., Germany)

                            
"Thank you, Chair. Germany strongly supports this essential resolution.

                            
(Moral/Rights-Based Argument) First, digital privacy is a fundamental human right in the 21st century. Currently, 3 billion people worldwide lack basic legal protection for their personal data. This resolution establishes universal minimum standards, ensuring all citizens have control over their digital identity.

                            
(Implementation Argument) Second, the explicit consent requirement addresses a critical problem. Companies collect vast amounts of personal data through confusing terms of service. This clause empowers individuals with meaningful choice.

                            
Some may argue this stifles innovation, but privacy and progress are not mutually exclusive. The digital economy can thrive while respecting human rights. We urge your support. Thank you."

                        

                        

                            
‚ùå Speech AGAINST (e.g., United States)

                            
"Thank you, Chair. The United States opposes this resolution, though we share the goal of protecting privacy.

                            
(Sovereignty Argument) First, this resolution imposes a one-size-fits-all approach on nations with vastly different legal systems. Member states should determine their own balance between privacy and innovation.

                            
(Unintended Consequences Argument) Second, the "right to be forgotten" is fundamentally flawed. It conflicts with freedom of expression and press freedom. Should historical records be deleted? This clause creates dangerous censorship potential.

                            
(Sovereignty/Scope Argument) Third, the proposed international enforcement agency violates national sovereignty. No UN body should have authority to impose fines on private companies within member states. We need privacy protection, but not at the cost of innovation, sovereignty, and free expression. Vote against. Thank you."

Speech Analysis & Argument Types

                

                    

                        
Key Analysis Tips

                        
    
                            
  • Read the *entire* resolution carefully. Focus on operative clauses.
    • 
                            
  • Identify the strongest points‚Äîpick 2-3 arguments maximum.
    • 
                            
  • Use specific evidence (numbers, examples, real consequences).
    • 
                            
  • Align with your country's position and national interests.
    • 
                            
  • Anticipate the opposition and address their strongest argument.
    • 
                            
  • Practice timing: most THIMUN speeches are 60-90 seconds.
    • 
                            
  • End strong with a clear call to action (e.g., "We urge a vote in favor.")
    • 
                        

                    

                    

                        
Common Argument Types

                        
    
                            
  • Moral/Rights-Based: "This is a fundamental human right..."
    • 
                            
  • Economic: "The cost of this program is..." or "The economic benefit will be..."
    • 
                            
  • Scientific: "The data from the IPCC clearly shows..."
    • 
                            
  • Past Precedent: "A similar program succeeded in..." or "This failed in the 1990s..."
    • 
                            
  • Sovereignty: "This resolution infringes on the national sovereignty of member states."
    • 
                            
  • Implementation: "The plan is unworkable because it lacks..."
    • 
                            
  • Financial: "The funding mechanism is unclear/unsustainable."
    • 
                            
  • Unintended Consequences: "While well-intentioned, this will actually harm..."
    • 
                            
  • Better Alternative: "We should vote this down and instead focus on..."
    • 
                            
  • Scope/Mandate: "This issue is outside the mandate of this committee."
    • 
                            
  • Vagueness: "The language in clause 3 is too vague to be enforceable."
