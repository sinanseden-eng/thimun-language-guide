1Ô∏è‚É£ Point of Information (to the Speaker) ‚ñº

Purpose: Ask questions to the speaker who currently has the floor, but ONLY if they have indicated willingness to yield to points of information.

When to use: After a speech when the speaker yields to points of information. Must be formulated as a question (a short introductory statement may precede the question).

‚ö†Ô∏è THIMUN Rule: Cannot interrupt a speaker. Follow-up questions from the same questioner are usually not in order.
üìç Example: Asking for Elaboration
Chair: "Thank you, delegate of India. To what point do you yield your time?"
Delegate of India: "The delegate of India yields to Points of Information."
Chair: "Are there any Points of Information on the floor?"
(You raise your placard and are recognized)
You (Kenya): "Point of Information to the speaker. The delegate of India mentioned a new fund. Could the delegate please elaborate on how this fund would be financed?"
Delegate of India: "The delegate thanks the delegate of Kenya for the question. The fund would be financed through..."
2Ô∏è‚É£ Point of Information to the Chair ‚ñº

Purpose: Ask the Chair questions about any matter related to the committee's business or for clarification on non-procedural issues.

When to use: When you need clarification from the Chair about committee business, timing, or content matters. Cannot interrupt a speaker.

üìç Example: Asking About Time
Chair: "We will now move to debate on the draft resolution. Are there any points or motions?"
(You raise your placard)
You (Egypt): "Point of Information to the Chair."
Chair: "The delegate of Egypt."
You: "Could the Chair inform the committee of the debate time allocated for this resolution?"
Chair: "The Chair proposes 30 minutes of debate time. Seeing no objections, it is set."
3Ô∏è‚É£ Point of Parliamentary Enquiry ‚ñº

Purpose: Ask the Chair questions *specifically* concerning the rules of procedure.

When to use: When you are confused about the rules (e.g., "Is a motion to... in order?"). Cannot interrupt a speaker.

üìç Example: Motion Clarification
Chair: "Thank you, delegate. Are there any points or motions?"
(You raise your placard)
You (Nigeria): "Point of Parliamentary Enquiry."
Chair: "Yes, delegate of Nigeria?"
You: "Is it in order to move the Previous Question at this time?"
Chair: "That is correct, delegate. A motion to close debate would be in order."
4Ô∏è‚É£ Point of Order ‚ñº

Purpose: Call attention to a procedural error (when the rules of procedure are being violated by the Chair or a delegate).

When to use: Only when there is a clear violation of procedure. This is serious and should be used sparingly. Cannot interrupt a speaker (unless it's about voting).

‚ö†Ô∏è THIMUN Rule: Points of Order may ONLY relate to procedural matters, not speech content.
üìç Example: Incorrect Debate Time
Chair: "The Chair proposes 20 minutes of debate time for this amendment."
(You raise your placard)
You (Japan): "Point of Order."
Chair: "State your point, delegate of Japan."
You: "According to the conference rules, debate on amendments is limited to a maximum of 10 minutes."
Chair: "The point is well taken. The Chair stands corrected. Debate time is set at 10 minutes."
5Ô∏è‚É£ Point of Personal Privilege ‚ñº

Purpose: Address issues of personal comfort (e.g., room temperature, noise) that prevent you from participating.

When to use: This is the ONLY point that can interrupt a speaker, but *only* if the speech is inaudible.

‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL THIMUN Rule: It may NOT refer to the content of any speech.
üìç Example 1: Inaudible Speaker (CAN INTERRUPT)
Delegate of Italy: (speaking very quietly) "...and the delegate believes..."
You (Argentina): "Point of Personal Privilege!"
Chair: "The speaker will pause. State your point, delegate of Argentina."
You: "The delegate cannot hear the speaker. The delegate kindly requests that the speaker speak more loudly."
Chair: "Delegate of Italy, please speak into the microphone. You may continue."

‚öñÔ∏è Motions & Yields

ü§ù Yielding (Giving Up Your Time) ‚ñº

After your speech, the Chair will ask how you yield your time. You have three main options.

Option 1: To the Chair (Most Common)
You: "The delegate yields the floor back to the Chair."
What it means: You are done and do not want to answer questions.
Option 2: To Questions
You: "The delegate yields to Points of Information."
What it means: You are opening the floor for other delegates to ask you questions.
Option 3: To Another Delegate
You: "The delegate yields the floor to the delegate of Argentina."
What it means: You give the rest of your time to another delegate (must be pre-arranged). THIMUN Rule: This can only be done once consecutively. The next delegate must yield back to the Chair.
üó≥Ô∏è Motion to Close Debate (Previous Question) ‚ñº

Purpose: To end the debate on the current topic (resolution or amendment) and move immediately to a vote.

üìç Example: Closing Debate
Chair: "Are there any points or motions on the floor?"
You (Germany): "Honourable Chair, the delegate of Germany moves the Previous Question."
Chair: "That motion is in order. Are there any seconds?"
(Other Delegates): "Second!"
Chair: "Are there any objections?"
(Another Delegate): "Objection!"
Chair: "We will have two speakers for and two speakers against this motion..."
üóìÔ∏è Motion to Adjourn the Debate ‚ñº

Purpose: To stop debate on the current topic (resolution) and "table" it, meaning it is set aside to be potentially debated later, after all other resolutions.

Rules: This motion is debatable (usually one speaker for, one against) and requires a simple majority to pass.

üìç Example: Tabling a Resolution
You (Argentina): "Honourable Chair, the delegate of Argentina motions to adjourn the debate on Resolution 1.1."
Chair: "That motion is in order. Are there any seconds? ... Objections? ... We will have one speaker for and one against this motion."
‚è±Ô∏è Motion to Extend Debate Time ‚ñº

Purpose: To add more time for debate on the current topic (resolution or amendment).

Rules: This motion is not debatable and is decided at the Chair's discretion. It requires a "second."

üìç Example: Extending Time
Chair: "Debate time has elapsed. Are there any points or motions?"
You (Canada): "Honourable Chair, the delegate motions to extend debate time by 10 minutes."
Chair: "Are there any seconds? ... Seeing seconds, the Chair will entertain this. We will move to a vote..."
üîÑ Motion to Reconsider a Resolution ‚ñº

Purpose: To bring a resolution that has already been passed or failed back to the floor for a new debate and vote.

Rules: This can only be done after all other resolutions on the agenda have been debated. It is not debatable and requires a two-thirds (2/3) majority to pass.

üìã Other Motions & Objections ‚ñº
Motion to Refer to Another Forum
Purpose: Used if a clause is outside the committee's power (e.g., uses "Demands" in the General Assembly). It's discouraged; chairs prefer amending the clause (e.g., to "Urges the Security Council to...").
Chair: "Are there any points or motions on the floor?"
You (Peru): "Honourable Chair, the delegate of Peru motions to refer this resolution to the Security Council, as operative clause 2 uses language such as 'Demands' which is outside the purview of the General Assembly."
Chair: "That motion is in order. This motion is debatable. Is the delegate of Peru willing to speak for their motion?"
Withdrawing a Resolution
Rule: The main submitter can motion to withdraw their resolution, but it requires *all* co-submitters to agree to it.
Chair: "Are there any points or motions on the floor?"
Delegate of Japan (Main Submitter): "Honourable Chair, after hearing the debate, the delegate of Japan motions to withdraw Resolution 1.1 from the floor."
Chair: "That motion is in order. The Chair asks all co-submitters of Resolution 1.1: are there any objections to withdrawing this resolution? ... Seeing no objections from the co-submitters, Resolution 1.1 is withdrawn."
Objection to the Main Motion
Purpose: To object to the entire resolution being debated, often on grounds that it violates national sovereignty. It is strongly discouraged as it's destructive to debate. Requires a 2/3 majority.
Chair: "The next resolution for debate is Resolution 1.2. Are there any points or motions on the floor?"
You (China): "Honourable Chair, the delegate of China objects to the main motion."
Chair: "An objection to the main motion has been raised. This is a motion to *not* debate this resolution. Delegate of China, you have the floor for a short speech on your objection."
You (China): "The delegate objects on the grounds that this resolution is a clear violation of national sovereignty..."
Chair: "Thank you. This motion is not debatable and requires a two-thirds majority to pass. We will now vote on the objection... All those in favor of *not* debating this resolution, please raise your placards..."

üîë Key Rules & Terms

üó≥Ô∏è Voting Procedure: Who Can Vote? ‚ñº

A critical distinction exists between "procedural" and "substantive" votes.

Substantive Votes (Resolutions & Amendments)
Only Member States (countries) can vote on resolutions and amendments.
Observers (e.g., NGOs like the Red Cross, or non-member states) CANNOT vote on substantive matters.
Procedural Votes (Motions)
ALL delegates (Member States *and* Observers) can vote on procedural motions.
Example: An NGO (Observer) cannot vote on an amendment, but *can* vote on a "Motion to Extend Debate Time" for that amendment.
üì£ Right of Reply ‚ñº

Purpose: This is not a "Point" but a formal right. A delegate can request a Right of Reply if their country or delegation has been insulted by a speaker.

Rules: You must send a note to the Chair requesting it. The Chair will grant it at their discretion, usually after the speech. It is not meant to debate policy, only to respond to the insult.

üìç Example: Responding to an Insult
Delegate of X: "...which is why the warmongering policy of Country Y is destabilizing the region."
(You, Delegate of Y, send a note to the Chair)
Chair: "Thank you, delegate of X. The Chair has received a request for a Right of Reply from the delegate of Y. You have the floor for 30 seconds."
You (Y): "The delegate of Y wishes to state that our policies are for self-defense, not "warmongering," and we find such language unhelpful to diplomatic debate."
üìã Roll Call & Quorum ‚ñº
Roll Call
What it is: The attendance check at the beginning of every session.
Chair: "The committee will come to order. We will now begin Roll Call. Delegates, please respond 'Present' or 'Present and Voting'..."
Quorum
What it is: The minimum number of delegates (usually one-third) that must be present for formal debate to begin and for votes to be valid. The Chair establishes quorum during the first roll call.

üí° THIMUN Pro Tips

  • Respect the Chair: You must respect the decisions of the Chair at all times. Their rulings are final.
  • Stand Up: You must always stand when speaking, whether giving a speech or raising a point.
  • Be Diplomatic: Always be courteous. You may attack a country's *policy*, but never the *delegate*.
  • One Thing at a Time: The committee only debates one resolution at a time, and only one amendment to that resolution at a time.
  • No Interruptions: A speaker may *never* be interrupted, except for an audibility-related Point of Personal Privilege.
  • Voting is Sacred: Once the Chair has announced the start of voting, no interruptions are allowed except for a Point of Order related to the vote itself.
